Computer Science Curriculum

In Summer 2022, I was a computer science research assistant at Williams College. Along with three other undergraduates, I developed a portable and accessible computer science curriculum for children, called Technologeph. The curriculum content is on a website that we programmed. It is free to access and download our worksheets, activities, slideshows, and lesson plans. We created the curriculum because there is an absence of effective computer science programs for young students. Most existing curriculums lack cohesion, accessibility, and content.

Furthermore, computer science as a discipline and industry lacks diversity. Although technology occupations are among the fastest-growing careers (Bureau of Labor Statistics), only 19% of STEM graduates are women and just 24% of computing jobs are held by women (DataProt). This is due, in part, because gender stereotypes related to STEM ability emerge at a young age (McGuire et al.). These stereotypes are then enforced by imbalanced gender participation in schools and later in the workforce. My group and I stipulate that early exposure to computer science education can help strike down gender stereotypes and inspire more girls to pursue their STEM interests. Additionally, computer science education is obliquely available to high-income school districts with less diverse student populations. Our portable and free-to-access curriculum is intended to broaden computer science accessibility to a more diverse group of students.

While the curriculum teaches students how to program, it more importantly teaches them how to think like a programmer. Programmers employ computational thinking to solve problems efficiently. Computational thinking involves decomposing problems into smaller tasks, recognizing patterns, designing algorithms, and debugging and refining those algorithms. Whether or not a student decides to pursue a career in computer science, the logic and reasoning skills cultivated by computational thinking are invaluable.
